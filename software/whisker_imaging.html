Whisker Imaging

Lighting

LED Light Source

There are several important features of our light source for whisker imaging: 1) We need the light to be sufficiently intense so that short exposure times can be used to capture the fast moving whiskers; 2) The light source should be collimated to best emphasize the contrast between the whiskers and the background; and 3) The light source should be in the near infrared spectrum so that it is invisible to the animal.

We first need an infrared light source that can provide sufficient intensity. Near infrared (NIR) light emitting diodes (LEDs) are easy to use and cost effective. Remember that LEDs are nonlinear electronic devices which take a roughly fixed voltage, but variable current. The amount of light (and heat) generated by the diode will be proportional to the current input.

There are different options for high-power NIR LEDs on the market. The classic ones referenced in whisker papers from the Svoboda family tree are from Roithner LaserTechnik in Austria. This is a good option for our EU friends. In the USA, there are several options available from large electronic suppliers like Digikey or Mouser. I have personally used the Osram LZ4 parts in both 850nm and 940nm wavelength with success:

There are a few features to highlight about these devices: 1) They are actually 4 LEDs packaged together, consequently they require approximately ~12 volts to run; 2) they come attached to a star pcb board, which can be a little tricky to solder; 3) the input current modulates the brightness; and 4) even with low currents, they are going to generate a *lot* of heat. Note that theese LEDs are rated for ~1 A, so even at 10% power, they are going to be generating on the order of 1 Watt (IV = 0.1 A * 12 V).

First, we need to be able to be able to turn the diodes on. It would be handy to have wires attached to the star PCB boards they come attached to. Most of these have 6 contact pins, but most are duplicates (the others are for cases where there may be different color LEDs which can be controlled individually, whereas we have 4 identical LEDs all wired together). The ground for these boards are quite large and made to dissipate heat, which can make soldering a little tricky. I generally solder with the back of the PCB on a hot plate turned to ~50 C to keep the baseline temperature higher, and allow the solder to stick better to the contact pads.

Next, we want to attach a heat sink to dissipate the heat this LED will generate. Many shapes and sizes of heat sinks can be purchased to attach to the LED; however, I have yet to find one that is an ideal shape to work with other components we might have in our neurophysiology rigs, or parts I will describe later for light collimation. A heatsink is just a block of metal, so we can alternatively glue our LED board to something that is the ideal shape.
